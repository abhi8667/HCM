\begin{abstract}
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is the most common inherited heart muscle disorder and a well-known cause of sudden cardiac death in otherwise healthy young individuals. Routine sequencing now routinely surfaces missense substitutions across nine sarcomeric genes, but figuring out which ones actually matter clinically is still largely unsolved. Families waiting on cascade screening have no clear path forward.

We tackled this by building a two-model pathogenicity prediction framework designed from the ground up to avoid the data leakage problems that silently inflate reported metrics in this field. On the feature side, we stripped out any variable that could be traced back to ACMG/AMP curation. On the evaluation side, we used Leave-One-Gene-Out (LOGO) cross-validation so the models had to generalize to gene families they had never seen during training.

Training used 1,954 high-confidence labeled missense variants (1,441 pathogenic; 513 benign) across the nine sarcomeric genes, with the framework then applied to 4,524 unresolved Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS). We built two classifiers: a Baseline Random Forest using 51 hand-crafted biophysical descriptors, and a Two-Tower Hybrid Neural Network combining those descriptors with 1,280-dimensional differential embeddings from a frozen 650M-parameter ESM-2 protein language model.

Under LOGO evaluation, the Baseline RF hit a mean AUPRC of \textbf{0.886} (95\% CI across all genes), beating EVE~(0.791), AlphaMissense~(0.800), REVEL~(0.842), MetaRNN~(0.849), and CardioBoost~(0.876) on seven of nine genes. We used Platt Scaling to align the neural networks raw prediction with real clinical probabilities, achieving a Brier score of 0.145 on held-out \textit{TNNT2} data. To help understand what the model was learning, per residue \textit{in silico} mutagenesis (ISM) heat maps were created. Interestingly these highlight non vulnerable hotspots. This includes myosin converter domain and the actin binding cleft even though the model had no access to 3-D structural information. Overall, these results help demonstrate that disease focused and leakage free approach can help capture meaning aspects of sarcomere biology. More importantly, it does so well enough to be practically useful in prioritizing variants of uncertain significance (VUS) in a clinical setup.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Proteins, Mutations, and Variant Interpretation}

Proteins are the workhorses of the cell. Made from long chains of amino acids that fold into intricate three dimensional shapes, they handle everything from speeding up chemical reactions to giving cells their structures to passing along signals. But when a missense mutation swaps one amino acid for another, the impact can vary widely from no effect at all to a complete breakdown of functions. All of this depends on where the change happens and how does it affect the local chemistry.

Experimental approaches like deep mutational scanning \cite{fowler2014deep} give you a real answer. But this approach is highly expensive and slow relative to the rate at which the variants are discovered. This is the reason why computational prediction tools have become so central in clinical genomics. They work by exploiting evolutionary conservation, structural context, and machine learning to assign pathogenicity scores, working within the 5 tier ACMG/AMP classification system \cite{richards2015}---this system helps in classifying: pathogenic, likely pathogenic, uncertain significance, likely benign, or benign. Despite many years of progress on general purpose predictors, inconsistency remains in performance within a narrow disease specific gene panels. This gap is what this paper tries to address.

\subsection{Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy: Clinical and Genetic Context}

HCM roughly affects 1 in 200 to 500 people and usually follows autosomal dominant inheritance \cite{maron2013}. The main hallmark of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is a thickened wall of the left ventricle that cannot be explained by high blood pressure or valve disease. Clinically HCM is unpredictable. Some people live their whole lives without any major symptoms, while others with HCM experience sudden cardiac death during day to day activity---sometimes as their first sign of the disease. Being common in people yet often silent is exactly why accurate interpretation of genetic variance matters so much for families at risk. 

At its core, HCM is the disease of the basic unit---the sarcomere---that makes the heart muscles contract. About 60\% of patients with genetic diagnosis carry harmful variants in sarcomere genes. Two genes, \textit{MYH7} and \textit{MYBPC3}, account for more than half of the known cases, while others are spread across genes like \textit{TNNT2}, \textit{TNNI3}, \textit{TPM1}, \textit{ACTC1}, \textit{MYL2}, \textit{MYL3}, and \textit{TNNC1}. These 9 genes form the panel for our study in this paper.

One complication is that the same mutations can lead to very different outcomes for different people. That means looking at the level of risk is not enough---you need to understand each variant on its own.

\subsection{The VUS Bottleneck in HCM}

The real clinical problem in HCM does not lie in the clearly pathogenic variants, which generally have segregation and functional data to back them up. The real problem is the large grey zone of missense substitutions that fall somewhere in between. Under ACMG/AMP guidelines \cite{richards2015}, variants without enough evidence to say whether a variant is safe or dangerous get labeled as Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS).

Across the nine genes in our study, we collected 8,137 missense observations---and more than 4,500 of them are currently tagged as VUS in ClinVar. That is over twice the number of variants with confident labels. For families at risk, a VUS result means no clear guidance and no way to move forward with cascade screening.

To our knowledge, no tool has yet been built and rigorously tested---without data leakage---to sort through the backlog. Filling this gap is exactly what we set out to do.

\subsection{Problem Statement and Approach}

\subsubsection{Label Circularity in Existing Predictors}
One issue that doesn't get enough attention in this field is label circularity. Many tools that predict whether a genetic variant is harmful are trained on features that were themselves derived from the same clinical workflow that produced the training labels. In other words, the models end up learning to copy the labeling process rather than the underlying biology. We took deliberate steps to avoid this problem both in how we engineered features and in how we evaluate performance.

\subsubsection{Our Approach}
To tackle these problems, we built a two model framework trained on 1,954 high confidence sarcomeric HCM variants. Importantly, we drew labels only from multi submitter ClinVar records, ensuring that the training data reflected consensus rather than single source judgment.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Proteome-Wide Pathogenicity Prediction}

EVE \cite{frazer2021eve} is an impressive resource: it covers 36 million variants across 3,219 genes. But when it comes to hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), it has a key limitation. EVE doesn't capture the specific domain architecture of sarcomeric proteins. For example, it treats the motor domain of MYH7 just like any other conserved segment, missing the disease-specific structural logic that actually drives HCM pathology. 

AlphaMissense \cite{cheng2023alphamissense} takes a different path. It fine-tunes the AlphaFold2 architecture using allele frequencies from human and primate populations to build a proteome-wide classifier that covers about 89\% of all possible human missense variants. The structural detail baked into AlphaFold's representations gives it a clear advantage in principle.

REVEL \cite{ioannidis2016revel} is an ensemble method that combines scores from thirteen different tools (including SIFT, PolyPhen-2, MutPred, VEST, GERP++, phyloP, and others) using a random forest trained on ClinVar. It's widely used in clinical pipelines through dbNSFP and Ensembl VEP. The catch is that its performance depends heavily on how many ClinVar labels a gene already has---genes with lots of historical annotations look easy, while genes with sparse records look hard. That pattern reflects label density, not the true molecular complexity of the problem.

MetaRNN \cite{li2022metarnn} takes a different approach, using gated recurrent units to combine 28 functional scores with a local sequence window of $\pm 1$ codon. The authors deliberately excluded ClinVar-trained sub-scores like MPC and ClinPred to avoid inflating performance through overlapping training and test variants---a problem they call Type I circularity. That's a genuine methodological improvement. However, MetaRNN still doesn't enforce gene-level separation between training and test splits, so the protein-level homology issue described by Grimm et al. (Type II circularity) remains.

Across all four tools we reviewed, the same core weakness stands out: none were designed with gene-held-out generalization in mind, and none encode the specific structural features---like domain membership, interface proximity, or mechanical coupling---that make sarcomere biology distinctive.

\subsection{Disease-Specific Tools for Inherited Cardiac Conditions}

CardioBoost \cite{zhang2021cardioboost} is the closest precedent to our work. It's a gradient-boosted classifier built specifically for rare missense variants in inherited cardiomyopathies and arrhythmias, using a gnomAD frequency cutoff of 0.1\% to minimize benign contamination. On its internal validation set, it reported an AUPRC of 91\% for cardiomyopathy variants---an impressive result. CardioBoost is available through the CardioClassifier web platform and covers most major sarcomeric HCM genes, though TNNC1 is missing from its precomputed scores.

The main methodological issue is that CardioBoost validated performance using random variant-level train-test splits. As we show in Section \ref{sec:results}, this choice substantially overstates accuracy compared to gene-held-out evaluation.

\subsection{Data Resources}

Building a leakage-free HCM training set required pulling from three public repositories:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ClinVar \cite{landrum2016clinvar} for pathogenicity labels
    \item UniProt Swiss-Prot \cite{uniprot2023} for structural and sequence annotations
    \item gnomAD \cite{karczewski2020} for population allele frequencies
\end{itemize}
Section \ref{sec:data} describes the retrieval pipeline and how each resource feeds into the framework.

\subsection{Machine Learning for Variant Pathogenicity and the Leakage Problem}

\subsubsection{The Case for Disease-Specific Machine Learning}
It's no surprise that CardioBoost outperforms proteome-wide tools on cardiac variants. Training on disease-relevant examples and engineering features that reflect the biology---like sarcomere domain boundaries, proximity to force-transmission interfaces, and cardiac-specific evolutionary patterns---captures signals that cross-gene averages simply miss. Proteome-wide models are powerful, but they're blunt instruments for targeted panel interpretation.

ESM-2 \cite{lin2023esm2} adds something genuinely new. It's a transformer trained on hundreds of millions of protein sequences via masked language modeling. Its representations carry rich structural and evolutionary information without requiring multiple sequence alignments at inference time. The key property we use is that mutations cause perturbations in ESM-2 embedding space that reflect evolutionary fitness cost---independent of clinical labels. This independence is crucial for avoiding leakage.

\subsubsection{The Data Leakage Problem in Clinical Genomics}
Leakage is common enough in ML-based science that it deserves careful attention here. In variant pathogenicity prediction, Grimm et al. identified two forms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Type I}: the same variant appears in both training and test sets
    \item \textbf{Type II}: different variants from the same protein appear in both splits
\end{itemize}
Both inflate reported metrics and mask poor generalization.

Feature leakage is another issue. If input features include population allele frequencies, ACMG classification fields, or clinical assertion counts, the model can end up learning the curation process rather than the biology. We address both dimensions at once: all ACMG-derivable features are excluded from our input vectors, and model evaluation uses LOGO cross-validation to enforce complete gene-level separation.

\section{Proposed Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Data Curation and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:data}

We built the training dataset by aggregating and filtering clinical and functional annotations from three public repositories:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ClinVar} provided all clinical pathogenicity labels. We restricted training to variants with a two-star or higher review status, meaning at least two independent submitters agreed on the classification. The ClinVar VUS set across our nine-gene panel became the inference cohort.
    \item \textbf{UniProt Swiss-Prot} supplied manually curated structural and sequence annotations for the nine proteins: canonical sequences, domain boundaries, secondary structure assignments (helix, strand, coil), disordered regions, binding sites, post-translational modifications, and active sites. Both UniProt and ClinVar data were retrieved programmatically through the EBI Proteins API.
    \item \textbf{gnomAD} was used to populate the benign class. Variants with a global allele frequency above 0.1\% were treated as benign proxies, counteracting the pathogenic-heavy reporting bias in ClinVar.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Embeddings}

We built two feature sets: one capturing local biophysical properties of each substitution, and one capturing evolutionary context from a protein language model.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Leakage Prevention.}
Six columns were excluded from the feature matrix before modeling: \texttt{pop\_freq}, \texttt{clin\_sig}, \texttt{review\_status}, \texttt{disease}, \texttt{sources}, and \texttt{genomic\_loc}. These fields either encode ACMG/AMP criteria directly or are co-derived from the same clinical workflows that produced the training labels. Keeping them would risk teaching the model database patterns instead of molecular biology. After exclusion, the tabular feature vector is a 51-dimensional mix of numeric and boolean values.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Biophysical Descriptors.}
Each variant is described by a 51-dimensional vector, organized into five groups:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Physicochemical distance:} Grantham distance between wild-type and mutant residues, based on composition, polarity, and molecular volume.
    \item \textbf{Charge and size alterations:} Explicit encoding of wild-type and mutant charge classes ($-1, 0, +1$) and size classes ($1, 2, 3$), plus signed deltas ($\Delta\mathrm{charge}$, $\Delta\mathrm{size}$).
    \item \textbf{Domain-level context:} Flags for whether the mutation falls in a motor domain, coiled-coil segment, regulatory zone, or other annotated functional region.
    \item \textbf{Functional site proximity:} Indicators for proximity to active sites, ligand-binding sites, metal-binding sites, phosphorylation targets, and local secondary structure type (helix, strand, coil).
    \item \textbf{Relative position:} Normalized sequence coordinate ($\rho = \mathrm{position} / \mathrm{protein length}$).
\end{itemize}

Where UniProt annotations were missing, we imputed zeros---a conservative choice that avoids over-interpreting unannotated regions.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Differential ESM-2 Embeddings.}
To bring in evolutionary context, we extracted a differential embedding $\Deltaemb_i$ from a frozen ESM-2 model \cite{lin2023esm2} (\texttt{esm2\_t33\_650M\_UR50D}, 33 Transformer layers). For each variant, we ran both the wild-type and mutant sequences through the frozen encoder $f_\theta$, then mean-pooled across positions:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{e}_i^{\mathrm{WT}}  = \mathrm{MeanPool}\bigl(f_\theta(s_i^{\mathrm{WT}})\bigr),\quad
  \mathbf{e}_i^{\mathrm{MUT}} = \mathrm{MeanPool}\bigl(f_\theta(s_i^{\mathrm{MUT}})\bigr).
\end{equation}
The mutation perturbation vector is simply the difference:
\begin{equation}
  \Deltaemb_i = \mathbf{e}_i^{\mathrm{MUT}} - \mathbf{e}_i^{\mathrm{WT}} \;\in\;\mathbb{R}^{1280}.
  \label{eq:delta_emb}
\end{equation}
Subtracting the wild-type representation removes the background signal shared across the protein family, leaving only the shift attributable to the specific mutation \cite{frazer2021eve}. Freezing the ESM-2 weights ensures these embeddings are statistically independent of our clinical training labels. All 1,954 precomputed embeddings are stored as a $1954 \times 1280$ float32 array in \texttt{data/esm2\_delta\_embeddings.npy}.

\subsection{Two-Tower Hybrid Neural Network}

We designed a dual-input network to combine tabular biophysical descriptors with high-dimensional ESM-2 embeddings. Rather than flattening both inputs into a single vector, we route them through separate projection layers first---a late-fusion design that lets each modality contribute independently before the final prediction.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Architecture Overview.}
The \textsc{HybridHCMModel} has two parallel linear projection pathways (``towers''), one per input type. Both towers output to a shared hidden dimension $h = 32$, and their outputs are concatenated and passed through a prediction head. Dropout ($p_\mathrm{drop} = 0.3$) is applied after every activation.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Tabular Feature Tower.}
The tabular vector $\xtab_i \in \mathbb{R}^{51}$ is projected via:
\begin{equation}
  \ztab_i = \mathrm{Dropout}\!\left(
      \mathrm{BN}\!\left(
        \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(W_\mathrm{tab}\,\xtab_i + \mathbf{b}_\mathrm{tab}\right)
      \right)
    \right),
  \label{eq:tab_tower}
\end{equation}
where $W_\mathrm{tab}\in\mathbb{R}^{h\times 51}$, $\mathbf{b}_\mathrm{tab}\in\mathbb{R}^h$, and BN is batch normalization with learnable scale and shift.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{ESM-2 Embedding Tower.}
The differential embedding $\xesm_i = \Deltaemb_i \in \mathbb{R}^{1280}$ passes through an identical projection:
\begin{equation}
  \zesm_i = \mathrm{Dropout}\!\left(
      \mathrm{BN}\!\left(
        \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(W_\mathrm{esm}\,\xesm_i + \mathbf{b}_\mathrm{esm}\right)
      \right)
    \right),
  \label{eq:esm_tower}
\end{equation}
where $W_\mathrm{esm}\in\mathbb{R}^{h\times 1280}$.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Fusion and Output Head.}
The two tower outputs are concatenated: $\zfus_i = [\ztab_i\,;\,\zesm_i] \in \mathbb{R}^{2h}$. A final linear block with batch normalization and dropout produces a scalar pathogenicity estimate:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{h}_i &= \mathrm{Dropout}\!\left(\mathrm{BN}\!\left(\mathrm{ReLU}(W_f\,\zfus_i + \mathbf{b}_f)\right)\right),
                  \quad W_f\in\mathbb{R}^{h\times 2h}, \label{eq:fusion}\\
  \hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{unc}} &= \sigma\!\left(w_o^\top\mathbf{h}_i + b_o\right),
                  \quad w_o\in\mathbb{R}^h, \label{eq:output}
\end{align}
with $\sigma(x)=(1+e^{-x})^{-1}$. The uncalibrated output is subsequently corrected with Platt scaling (Section~\ref{sec:platt}).

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Class-Balanced Binary Cross-Entropy Loss.}
With 1,441 pathogenic and 513 benign variants (roughly 2.8:1), we weight samples inversely by class frequency:
\begin{equation}
  w_i = \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{N}{2N_1} & \text{if } y_i = 1,\\[6pt]
    \dfrac{N}{2N_0} & \text{if } y_i = 0,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
giving a weighted BCE objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{BCE} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
    w_i\!\left[y_i \log \hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{unc}} +
    (1-y_i)\log(1-\hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{unc}})\right].
  \label{eq:bce}
\end{equation}
We used AdamW \cite{loshchilov2019adamw} with $\eta=0.003$ and weight decay $\lambda=10^{-4}$, training for $T=40$ epochs on the full training batch.

\subsection{Baseline Random Forest Classifier}

We included a Random Forest \cite{breiman2001rf} to establish whether a simpler ensemble model can make good use of the ESM-2 embeddings without a specialized fusion architecture. This also gives us a cleaner comparison point for interpreting what the neural network's two-tower design actually adds.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Ensemble Formulation.}
The forest has $B=200$ CART trees. Unlike the neural network, the RF takes a single flat input formed by concatenating both feature types:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}_i^{\mathrm{RF}} = \bigl[\xtab_i\,;\,\xesm_i\bigr]
    \in \mathbb{R}^{51+1280}.
  \label{eq:rf_input}
\end{equation}

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Node Split Criterion: Gini Impurity.}
Splits are chosen by maximizing the reduction in Gini impurity:
\begin{equation}
  I_G(t) = 1 - \sum_{k\in\{0,1\}} \hat{p}(k|t)^2,
  \label{eq:gini}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \Delta I_G = I_G(t) - \frac{n_L}{n}I_G(t_L) - \frac{n_R}{n}I_G(t_R).
\end{equation}
Tree depth is capped at $\mathrm{max\_depth}=10$.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Class-Balanced Bootstrap Sampling.}
Bootstrap draws are reweighted by inverse class frequency to handle the imbalance. The ensemble prediction averages class-1 probabilities across all trees:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{RF}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} T_b\!\left(\mathbf{x}_i^{\mathrm{RF}}\right).
  \label{eq:rf_pred}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Explainability via \textit{In Silico} Mutagenesis}

These raw pathogenicity scores are not enough for the researchers to make any conclusions or to understand why a variant scored the way it did. This is why we used \textit{In Silico} Mutagenesis (ISM) heatmaps analysis that scores all possible single-amino-acid substitutions within a target sequence window. 

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{ISM Landscape Generation.}
For a window of length $L$ and the 20-amino-acid alphabet $\mathcal{A}$, we generate variant contexts $s^{(j,a)}$ for every position $j$ and every alternative amino acid $a$. For each:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute $\Deltaemb^{(j,a)} = f_\theta(s^{(j,a)})_j - f_\theta(s^{\mathrm{WT}})_j \in \mathbb{R}^{1280}$.
  \item Update the tabular descriptor to reflect the new substitution (Grantham distance, charge and size deltas, binding site flags, etc.).
  \item Run the updated pair $(\xtab^{(j,a)}, \Deltaemb^{(j,a)})$ through the calibrated model. This produces an ISM matrix $M \in [0, 1]^{20\times L}$:
  \begin{equation}
    M_{a,j} = \hat{p}^{\,\mathrm{cal}}\!\left(\xtab^{(j,a)}, \Deltaemb^{(j,a)}\right).
    \label{eq:ism_matrix}
  \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
The wild-type entry at each column serves as the local reference. 

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Biomechanical mapping and heatmap visualisation.}
We show $M$ as a coolwarm heatmap with amino acids on rows and positions on columns. Vertical stripes of uniformly high pathogenicity indicate positions where almost any substitution is disruptive---structural bottlenecks. Horizontal bands represent substitution classes (charge reversals, large hydrophobic insertions) that are broadly destabilising regardless of position.

\section{Experimental Design and Evaluation}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Leave-One-Gene-Out Validation Protocol.}
We prevent Type-II homology leakage \cite{grimm2015circularity} by iteratively holding out every variant from one gene $g^* \in \mathcal{G}$ for testing:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{D}_\mathrm{train} &= \bigl\{(\mathbf{x}_i,\,y_i) : g_i \neq g^*\bigr\}, \\
  \mathcal{D}_\mathrm{test}  &= \bigl\{(\mathbf{x}_i,\,y_i) : g_i  =  g^*\bigr\}.
\end{align}
This repeats nine times, so every variant is test data exactly once, evaluated by a model that has never seen its gene family.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Bootstrap Confidence Intervals.}
We add 95\% nonparametric confidence intervals to AUPRC estimates via $B = 100$ bootstrap resamples:
\begin{equation}
  \overline{\mathrm{AUPRC}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{b\in\mathcal{B}}\mathrm{AUPRC}^{(b)}, \quad
  \mathrm{CI}_{95\%} = \bigl[Q_{2.5},\,Q_{97.5}\bigr].
\end{equation}
Resamples with fewer than two distinct classes are excluded for numerical stability.

\vspace{2mm}\noindent\textbf{Probability Calibration via Platt Scaling.}\label{sec:platt}
Neural networks trained with class-reweighted objectives tend to produce poorly calibrated probabilities. We fit a univariate logistic regression $\mathcal{C}$ to the in-fold training predictions $\hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{unc}}$ (clipped to $[10^{-7}, 1-10^{-7}]$):
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{cal}} = \mathcal{C}\!\left(\hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{unc}}\right) = \sigma\!\left(A\cdot\hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{unc}} + B\right),
  \label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}
using high inverse-regularization ($C=999$) to avoid over-shrinking. We measure calibration quality with the Brier score:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BS} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\hat{p}_i^{\,\mathrm{cal}} - y_i)^2,
  \label{eq:brier}
\end{equation}
and the Expected Calibration Error (ECE), computed as the sample-weighted mean absolute deviation between predicted probability and empirical positive rate across ten equal-width bins.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Value of Disease-Specific Language Modelling}

Despite that, our Baseline RF outperformed them by 9.5 and 8.6 mean AUPRC points under the same LOGO conditions. Adjusting the training data and feature set to match the target issue seems to be more important than just size, at least for a panel this specialized. 

The ablation experiment makes this situation more complex.

\subsection{Addressing the Two Leakage Channels}

Most previous work on variant pathogenicity prediction has not dealt with feature leakage and split-level leakage simultaneously. We removed six feature groups known to carry ACMG/AMP signal, and we held out entire gene families during evaluation rather than random variant subsets. The cost is somewhat lower headline numbers compared to what you get from standard random splits---but those inflated numbers are not informative about real clinical performance.

The REVEL and MetaRNN exceptions on \textit{MYH7} and \textit{TNNI3} ($\Delta$AUPRC around 0.07 in their favor) are worth a closer look. Both genes are the most heavily annotated in ClinVar, and both tools rely on population frequency features that correlate directly with ClinVar label density. Our LOGO setup removes those signals by design. That our models still reached AUPRC $>0.90$ on both genes purely from sequence and structural features is arguably the more meaningful result---it means the model is picking up on actual molecular patterns not just reflecting ClinVar label density.

\subsection{Translational Impact of ISM Heatmaps}

For clinical diagnostic labs, the ISM landscapes offer three concrete uses. First, positions that score high regardless of substitution identity are structural bottlenecks that likely warrant prioritized functional characterization. Second, by predicting which substitution classes are most destabilizing at each position, the heatmaps can guide the design of targeted mutagenesis experiments and reduce the number of constructs that need to be tested. Third, and most directly applicable to VUS reclassification: because our model was trained without ACMG-derived features, a high pathogenicity score constitutes independent computational evidence that can contribute to the PP3 evidence criterion. It is not circular.

\subsection{Understanding the Performance of the Random Forest vs.\ the Neural Network}

The RF outperforming the neural network on raw AUPRC was not what we initially expected, but it is less surprising when you consider the dataset size. We have 1,954 labeled variants in a 1,331-dimensional feature space. This is a high-dimensional, low-sample situation where tree ensembles usually perform well. Random Forests resist overfitting here because each tree sees only a part of the features, and the average of the ensemble smooths out the variance from individual trees. 

On the other hand, the issue of calibration is where the Two-Tower model consistently excels. Platt scaling directly optimizes the probability outputs for accuracy, while the RF probabilities come from leaf class proportions without an explicit calibration step. For clinical decision-making, where you need to understand not only the rank order of variants but also how much to trust each score, this difference really matters. A threshold of pˆ ≥ 0.90 has a clear meaning with a calibrated model. However, that same threshold is harder to interpret with uncalibrated RF outputs.

\subsection{Limitations}


There are four limitations we want to highlight. First, ESM-2 remained fixed throughout. Fine-tuning on sarcomeric sequences could improve the mutation signal, but it would need strict leakage controls to ensure HCM-labeled sequences do not leak into the representations. Second, we do not use 3-D structural coordinates anywhere in the pipeline. Adding geometry-aware features, such as contact maps from AlphaFold and residue-pair distances, could help capture long-range allosteric effects that sequence-level embeddings might overlook.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We built a two-model framework for HCM sarcomere variant pathogenicity prediction that systematically avoids both feature-level and split-level data leakage. The Baseline RF reached a mean AUPRC of \textbf{0.886} under LOGO cross-validation, outperforming EVE, AlphaMissense, REVEL, MetaRNN, and CardioBoost on seven of nine target genes. The Platt-calibrated Two-Tower model achieved a Brier score of \textbf{0.145} on the held-out \textit{TNNT2} cohort---well-calibrated enough to support clinical probability thresholds. The ISM heatmaps identified structurally sensitive residues at the myosin converter domain and troponin regulatory interface without requiring any 3-D coordinate inputs, which suggests the ESM-2 representations carry real structural and functional information from sequence alone.

More broadly, we think these results show that a carefully designed disease-specific model, evaluated honestly, can outperform larger proteome-wide systems when the target problem has well-defined structural logic. The practical output is a ranked list of 4,524 sarcomeric VUS, prioritized for functional validation, that diagnostic labs can use directly. The code, trained weights, and evaluation scripts are available in our project repository.
