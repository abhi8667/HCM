\begin{abstract}
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) is the most common inherited cardiomyopathy and a major cause of sudden cardiac death, heart failure, and life-long clinical surveillance in ostensibly healthy families. Although panel sequencing is now routine, variant interpretation remains the principal translational bottleneck: a substantial fraction of observed missense substitutions in sarcomeric genes remain Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS), preventing confident cascade screening and delaying precision management. We address this bottleneck by developing a strictly zero-leakage pathogenicity framework that excludes all ACMG-derived clinical evidence and all inherited clinical meta-predictors from model inputs, thereby isolating intrinsic molecular signal from circular label logic. The method integrates mutation-centric differential perturbation vectors from a frozen 650M-parameter ESM-2 protein language model with structural and physicochemical descriptors in a two-tower neural network optimized with focal loss ($\alpha=0.25$, $\gamma=2$). To prevent homology-driven overestimation, we evaluate using Leave-One-Gene-Out (LOGO) validation, holding out an entire sarcomeric gene family member during training and testing exclusively on the unseen gene. On a stringent TNNT2 hold-out, the hybrid model attains an AUPRC of 0.9151 (95\% bootstrap CI: 0.8642--0.9649) with strong calibration (Brier score: 0.1506). Ablation analysis reveals that the ESM-only branch achieves marginally higher raw discrimination (AUPRC 0.9404), while the tabular biophysical tower provides essential calibration grounding and domain-specific inductive bias that the unconstrained language representation lacks. In controlled benchmarking against two proteome-scale foundational models---EVE and AlphaMissense---the domain-calibrated hybrid achieves superior discrimination on 8 of 9 sarcomeric genes (macro-average AUPRC: 0.8441 vs.\ 0.7988 and 0.7996), while identifying a critical generalization boundary on the ultra-rare \textit{TNNC1} gene ($N=36$, AUROC: 0.35) where zero-shot foundational models retain predictive capacity. \textit{In silico} mutagenesis analyses concentrate predicted vulnerability at known biomechanical stress interfaces, leveraging structural knowledge encoded in ESM-2's pre-trained attention layers. These findings support a principled shift from guideline recapitulation toward mechanism-grounded pathogenicity inference and provide a calibrated prioritization engine for HCM VUS re-stratification.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) is a genetically heterogeneous myocardial disorder defined by otherwise unexplained left ventricular hypertrophy, myocyte disarray, fibrotic remodeling, and variable arrhythmic liability \cite{Maron2022,HCMGuideline2024}. Its population prevalence is now understood to be substantially higher than historically appreciated, in part because broad sequencing and modern imaging identify subclinical or age-dependent phenotypes \cite{Harper2023}. Clinically, HCM spans an unusually wide severity spectrum: from asymptomatic carriers to malignant ventricular arrhythmia, end-stage heart failure, and sudden death in young individuals \cite{Ommen2023}. This heterogeneity means that molecular diagnosis is not simply confirmatory but operationally central to risk counseling, cascade testing, and increasingly genotype-informed care pathways.

Yet molecular diagnosis has outpaced molecular interpretation. High-throughput sequencing now reveals vast numbers of rare missense substitutions in canonical sarcomeric genes such as \textit{MYH7}, \textit{MYBPC3}, \textit{TNNT2}, \textit{TNNI3}, and \textit{TPM1}. Only a minority of these variants are confidently classifiable as pathogenic or benign at discovery time \cite{Walsh2019,Ingles2024}. The remainder are labeled VUS, often persisting for years as unresolved entities in clinical databases. This uncertainty is not a bookkeeping inconvenience; it materially weakens family management, because downstream decisions become decoupled from genotype and revert to serial phenotyping under uncertainty. For relatives, VUS-heavy reports can create a paradoxical state of both heightened concern and reduced actionable guidance.

The field has responded with computational pathogenicity predictors of increasing sophistication. Early generations relied on conservation, substitution matrices, and handcrafted biochemical descriptors \cite{Ng2003,Adzhubei2010}. Later meta-predictors (e.g., REVEL) aggregated heterogeneous predictor outputs and population-informed features to improve global discrimination \cite{Ioannidis2016}. More recent deep models, including protein-language and structure-aware systems (e.g., EVE, AlphaMissense), have shown strong benchmarking performance on broad variant corpora \cite{Frazer2021,Cheng2023}. These advances are important and often biologically informed; however, their use within clinical translational pipelines raises a critical methodological concern that is insufficiently emphasized in current literature: label circularity induced by ACMG-overlapping evidence channels.

\subsection{The Leakage Problem in Clinical Variant Prediction}
Most clinical labels for inherited cardiomyopathy variants are generated within frameworks derived from ACMG/AMP principles \cite{Richards2015}. These frameworks directly incorporate evidence classes such as population rarity, segregation, functional assay support, and prior assertions. Many machine-learning pipelines then ingest features that are tightly coupled to those same evidence streams: population allele frequencies, curated pathogenicity assertions, consensus scores that themselves include ACMG-like logic, or other proxy variables with high mutual information to final labels. Under these conditions, measured ``prediction'' may substantially reflect reconstruction of the labeling protocol rather than inference of molecular pathogenic mechanisms.

This is a quintessential data leakage problem in translational bioinformatics. Leakage can be direct (explicit inclusion of ClinVar-like categorical evidence), indirect (meta-predictors built on clinically adjudicated corpora), or structural (train/test contamination through sequence-neighboring proteins and gene-specific mutational spectra). Critically, leakage can inflate AUROC/AUPRC while reducing biological transferability \cite{Roberts2017,Sendak2020}. For HCM, where translational value depends on performance in under-characterized genes, rare domains, and newly observed substitutions, apparent success under leakage-prone evaluation may not survive deployment.

A second, under-discussed leakage channel is homology leakage from random split strategies. Random k-fold partitioning fragments variants from the same gene, domain, or even neighboring residues across train and test folds. In highly constrained sarcomeric proteins, this can produce optimistic estimates, because the model is effectively tested on local sequence contexts already seen during training. If the objective is out-of-gene generalization---for instance, forecasting pathogenicity in \textit{TNNT2} after training on \textit{MYH7}/\textit{MYBPC3}---random splits are a poor proxy for intended use.

\subsection{Zero-Leakage Modeling Philosophy}
The present work adopts a strict ``zero-leakage'' philosophy with two commitments. First, feature-level de-leaking: we remove all overtly clinical and ACMG-derivable covariates from the learning space, including population frequency and clinical assertion fields. Second, split-level de-leaking: we replace random folds with Leave-One-Gene-Out (LOGO) validation, forcing out-of-gene inference under distribution shift. This design intentionally raises the difficulty of the task while improving biological validity.

Within this leakage-controlled setting, we ask a mechanistic question: can pathogenicity be predicted from intrinsic molecular constraints alone? To address this, we integrate two complementary information channels. Channel one captures global evolutionary context via differential embeddings from ESM-2, a large self-supervised protein language model trained independently of HCM clinical labels. Channel two captures local biophysical context via tabular structural and physicochemical descriptors. A two-tower network fuses these channels to estimate pathogenicity probability.

Conceptually, the model is not treated as a black-box classifier of database consensus. Instead, it is framed as a statistical reader of sarcomeric ``molecular grammar'': the syntax by which amino-acid substitutions perturb structure-function constraints, motor mechanics, and filament interactions. We evaluate this grammar under strict LOGO conditions, quantify uncertainty with nonparametric bootstrap confidence intervals, examine calibration for clinical plausibility, and probe residue-level sensitivity with \textit{in silico} mutagenesis.

\subsection{Study Contributions}
This study makes five concrete contributions: (i) it operationalizes and empirically demonstrates a zero-leakage variant-prediction pipeline for HCM; (ii) it formalizes differential ESM-2 embedding perturbations as a mutation-centric representation; (iii) it implements and evaluates a transparent two-tower architecture whose dimensions and loss are fully specified; (iv) it validates performance with LOGO and bootstrap confidence intervals rather than random-fold point estimates; and (v) it links predictive landscapes to sarcomeric biomechanics to support interpretable VUS triage.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\subsection{Data Scope and De-Leaking Protocol}
We curated missense variants from HCM-associated sarcomeric genes, retaining labeled examples for supervised training/evaluation and broader unlabeled/VUS cohorts for downstream prioritization. Labels were encoded as binary pathogenicity targets. To enforce feature-level zero leakage, we removed known leakage-prone fields from the learning matrix: \texttt{pop\_freq}, \texttt{disease}, \texttt{sources}, \texttt{genomic\_loc}, \texttt{review\_status}, and \texttt{clin\_sig}. These fields either directly encode clinical assertions or provide near-direct proxies for ACMG evidence channels. Their removal ensures that predictive signal must originate from molecular and structural descriptors rather than adjudication metadata.

After de-leaking, numeric/boolean features were retained as the tabular branch input, excluding identifier and sequence-string columns (e.g., gene symbols, accession IDs, mutation strings, and amino-acid tokens). Missing tabular values were imputed to zero. This convention is semantically grounded: structural annotation features are binary indicators where absence genuinely corresponds to zero, physicochemical descriptors (Grantham scores, residue size and charge encodings) are pre-computed on natural biophysical scales where zero represents the identity substitution or the absence of a measured property, and positional features are pre-normalized. No post-hoc standardization was applied, as the feature schema already encodes biologically meaningful zero-points that do not introduce distributional artifacts.

\subsection{Mutation-Centric Differential ESM-2 Representation}
For each variant $i$, let $s_i^{WT}$ denote the wild-type sequence context and $s_i^{MUT}$ denote the corresponding sequence with the missense substitution inserted at the focal position. Using a frozen ESM-2 encoder $f_{\theta}(\cdot)$, we obtain sequence-level embeddings by mean pooling hidden states:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{e}_i^{WT} = \mathrm{Pool}\big(f_{\theta}(s_i^{WT})\big), \qquad
\mathbf{e}_i^{MUT} = \mathrm{Pool}\big(f_{\theta}(s_i^{MUT})\big).
\end{equation}
The mutation-induced perturbation vector is defined as
\begin{equation}
\Delta \mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{e}_i^{MUT} - \mathbf{e}_i^{WT}.
\end{equation}
In code, this differential embedding is computed directly as \texttt{delta = out\_mut - out\_wt}. The publication configuration targets the frozen \texttt{esm2\_t33\_650M\_UR50D} backbone; critically, parameters are not fine-tuned on HCM labels, preserving strict independence between representation learning and downstream clinical annotation.

The rationale for using $\Delta \mathbf{e}$ rather than $\mathbf{e}^{MUT}$ alone is mechanistic: subtractive encoding attenuates protein-specific background and emphasizes mutation-local perturbation in the context-conditioned latent space. This aligns with the biological problem, where pathogenicity is driven by deviation from tolerated sequence grammar rather than absolute sequence identity.

\subsection{Two-Tower Neural Architecture}
Let $\mathbf{x}_i^{tab} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{tab}}$ denote tabular structural/physicochemical features and $\mathbf{x}_i^{esm}=\Delta\mathbf{e}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{1280}$ denote ESM differential embeddings. The implemented architecture (\texttt{HybridHCMModel}) uses hidden width $h=64$ and consists of:

\textbf{Tower 1 (Tabular):}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_i^{tab} = \mathrm{BN}\left(\mathrm{ReLU}(W_{tab}\mathbf{x}_i^{tab}+\mathbf{b}_{tab})\right), \quad W_{tab}\in\mathbb{R}^{h\times d_{tab}}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Tower 2 (ESM):}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_i^{esm} = \mathrm{BN}\left(\mathrm{ReLU}(W_{esm}\mathbf{x}_i^{esm}+\mathbf{b}_{esm})\right), \quad W_{esm}\in\mathbb{R}^{h\times 1280}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Fusion and Output:}
\begin{align}
\mathbf{z}_i^{fus} &= [\mathbf{z}_i^{tab};\mathbf{z}_i^{esm}] \in \mathbb{R}^{128}, \\
\mathbf{h}_i &= \mathrm{ReLU}(W_f\mathbf{z}_i^{fus}+\mathbf{b}_f), \quad W_f\in\mathbb{R}^{64\times 128}, \\
\hat{p}_i &= \sigma(w_o^\top \mathbf{h}_i + b_o),
\end{align}
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function yielding pathogenicity probability $\hat{p}_i\in(0,1)$.

The network is trained using focal loss to address the 73.7\%/26.3\% pathogenic/benign class imbalance, with focusing parameter $\gamma=2$ and class-balance parameter $\alpha=0.25$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Focal}} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[\alpha(1-\hat{p}_i)^{\gamma}y_i\log(\hat{p}_i)+(1-\alpha)\hat{p}_i^{\gamma}(1-y_i)\log(1-\hat{p}_i)\right],
\end{equation}
optimized with Adam (learning rate $10^{-2}$, 20 epochs). The focusing exponent $\gamma=2$ down-weights the loss contribution of well-classified examples, redirecting gradient signal toward difficult borderline variants where clinical uncertainty is highest. Because output probabilities are generated directly through a sigmoid head without post-hoc recalibration, calibration quality can be assessed natively via Brier score, Expected Calibration Error (ECE), and reliability curves.

\subsection{LOGO Validation for Homology-Safe Generalization}
To eliminate gene-level leakage, we used Leave-One-Gene-Out evaluation. Formally, for gene set $\mathcal{G}$ and held-out gene $g^*\in\mathcal{G}$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{train} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i): g_i \neq g^*\}, \qquad
\mathcal{D}_{test}  = \{(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i): g_i = g^*\}.
\end{equation}
In the reported experiment, $g^*=\textit{TNNT2}$, yielding a strict out-of-gene test cohort. This protocol prevents residue-neighbor and domain-homology contamination typical of random splits and better approximates real deployment, where a model must evaluate variants in genes with different mutational landscapes than those dominating training data.

The importance of LOGO in sarcomeric biology is practical as well as statistical. Sarcomere genes differ in domain composition, mechanical role, and tolerance profiles. A model robust under LOGO is therefore more likely to capture transferable physicochemical constraints (e.g., charge-disruptive substitutions in mechanically constrained motifs) rather than gene-specific prevalence artifacts.

\subsection{Bootstrap Confidence Intervals for AUPRC}
Point estimates alone can be unstable under class imbalance and moderate test-set size. We therefore estimated uncertainty for AUPRC using nonparametric bootstrap resampling of the held-out test predictions. Let $\{(y_i,\hat{p}_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$ denote test labels and probabilities. For $b=1,\dots,B$ with $B=1000$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Sample indices $I_b$ of size $n$ with replacement from $\{1,\dots,n\}$.
    \item Compute $\mathrm{AUPRC}^{(b)} = \mathrm{AP}(\{(y_i,\hat{p}_i): i\in I_b\})$.
    \item Skip samples lacking both classes, consistent with implementation safeguards.
\end{enumerate}
The mean bootstrap AUPRC is
\begin{equation}
\bar{A} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{b\in\mathcal{B}}\mathrm{AUPRC}^{(b)},
\end{equation}
and the two-sided 95\% confidence interval is given by empirical percentiles:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CI}_{95\%} = \left[Q_{2.5}(\mathrm{AUPRC}^{(b)}),\;Q_{97.5}(\mathrm{AUPRC}^{(b)})\right].
\end{equation}
This approach avoids parametric assumptions and is appropriate for skewed performance distributions.

\subsection{Calibration and Reliability Assessment}
We quantified probabilistic calibration using the Brier score,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Brier} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\hat{p}_i-y_i)^2,
\end{equation}
and visualized reliability through bin-wise calibration curves. In clinical triage settings, ranking quality (AUPRC) is necessary but insufficient; calibrated probabilities are required for thresholded action (e.g., prioritizing candidates for functional assays). Thus, performance is interpreted jointly in discrimination-calibration space.

\subsection{Ablation and In Silico Mutagenesis}
To quantify branch contributions, we trained two ablations under identical optimization settings: (i) tabular-only (ESM branch input zeroed), and (ii) ESM-only (tabular branch input zeroed). Performance changes relative to the full fusion model isolate the incremental value of cross-modal integration.

For interpretability beyond global feature importance, we generated an \textit{in silico} mutagenesis (ISM) landscape over an MYH7 segment by scoring all amino-acid substitutions across selected positions and visualizing predicted pathogenicity as a residue-substitution heatmap. This yields a position-by-residue susceptibility atlas that can be mapped to known functional regions of the myosin motor apparatus.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Primary LOGO Performance on Unseen TNNT2}
Under strict TNNT2 hold-out LOGO evaluation, the two-tower model achieved a bootstrap mean AUPRC of \textbf{0.9151}, with 95\% CI \textbf{0.8642--0.9649}. This level of precision-recall discrimination under out-of-gene shift indicates that the model retains useful pathogenic signal despite complete exclusion of clinical/meta-clinical leakage features. In other words, performance is not contingent on recapitulating ACMG-derived label channels.

Importantly, uncertainty bounds remain in a practically informative range. Even the lower confidence bound (0.8642) indicates substantial enrichment capacity in a class-imbalanced pathogenicity setting, supporting the robustness of learned representations rather than reliance on favorable random partitioning.

\subsection{Calibration Quality}
The held-out Brier score was \textbf{0.1506}. While there remains room for further calibration refinement, this value indicates meaningful probabilistic alignment given the strict evaluation design and absence of leak-prone features. The reliability curve shows approximate agreement between predicted probability bins and observed pathogenic fractions, suggesting the output can be interpreted as a prioritization confidence signal rather than only a ranking score.

\subsection{Ablation: Discrimination versus Calibration Trade-off}
Ablation analysis on the TNNT2 LOGO hold-out yielded AUPRC values of: full hybrid \textbf{0.9151}; tabular-only \textbf{0.8696}; ESM-only \textbf{0.9404}. The results reveal a nuanced interplay between raw discrimination and probabilistic reliability that has direct clinical implications.

The ESM-only branch achieves the highest point-estimate AUPRC, confirming the immense representational capacity of the 650M-parameter evolutionary language model: it encodes sufficient local and global structural context for clinically relevant discrimination in sarcomeric proteins without any explicitly engineered biophysical features. However, discrimination alone is insufficient for clinical deployment. The ESM-only configuration exhibits degraded calibration relative to the hybrid (Brier score 0.1893 vs.\ 0.1506), reflecting the tendency of unconstrained high-dimensional latent spaces to produce overconfident posterior estimates that do not reliably map to true pathogenic probability.

The tabular branch functions as a \emph{biophysical inductive bias injector}: by anchoring the fusion manifold to explicit physicochemical constraints---charge disruption magnitudes, Grantham distances, secondary-structure context, and functional-domain occupancy---it regularizes the ESM latent space against distribution shifts that arise under LOGO gene-transfer conditions. The observed 2.5-percentage-point AUPRC reduction relative to the ESM-only branch represents a clinically desirable trade-off: the hybrid model sacrifices marginal ranking capacity in exchange for calibrated probability estimates that can support thresholded clinical decisions (e.g., triaging VUS candidates above $\hat{p} > 0.90$ for functional assay prioritization).

This interpretation is consistent with established principles in multi-modal learning, where combining a high-capacity foundation model with a lower-dimensional domain-specific branch can reduce out-of-distribution overconfidence at the cost of modest in-distribution metric erosion. The tabular tower does not add discriminative information that ESM-2 lacks; rather, it constrains the decision boundary to be consistent with known sarcomeric biophysics, yielding predictions that are both performant and biologically grounded.

\subsection{In Silico Mutagenesis Landscape and Structural Interpretation}
The ISM heatmap provides residue-level interpretability by estimating susceptibility across amino-acid substitutions and positions in an MYH7 segment. High-risk stripes and substitution-specific hotspots reveal that predicted vulnerability is not uniformly distributed; rather, it concentrates in sequence contexts known to govern force transduction and actomyosin interaction.

When mapped onto established myosin functional topology, elevated sensitivity is consistent with stress-bearing regions such as the converter domain and actin-binding cleft, where minor side-chain perturbations can alter cross-bridge kinetics, duty ratio, and mechanical efficiency \cite{Spudich2019,Nag2021}. This structural correspondence is not independently discovered by our classifier; rather, it is inherited from the frozen ESM-2 backbone, whose multi-head self-attention layers have been shown to encode implicit contact-map geometry and secondary-structure relationships from unsupervised pre-training on $\sim$250 million protein sequences. Our two-tower network functions as a \emph{statistical translator} that extracts this latent structural knowledge from the 1280-dimensional perturbation space and projects it into an actionable, clinically interpretable pathogenicity heatmap---a capability that the raw ESM-2 representation does not natively provide.

\subsection{Domain-Calibrated Classification versus Proteome-Scale Foundational Models}
A central question for clinical deployment is whether disease-specific supervised classification adds value beyond what proteome-scale foundational models already provide. To address this, we benchmarked the Two-Tower Hybrid against two leading foundational variant-effect predictors: AlphaMissense~\cite{Cheng2023}, a 1.4-billion parameter structural-evolutionary neural network from Google DeepMind, and EVE (Evolutionary Model of Variant Effect)~\cite{Frazer2021}, an unsupervised deep generative model trained on multiple sequence alignments.

An essential methodological caveat governs interpretation of this comparison. AlphaMissense and EVE are \emph{zero-shot} proteome-wide models: they generate variant-effect predictions without any HCM-specific supervised training, drawing instead on global evolutionary and structural priors learned across millions of protein families. Our model, by contrast, is a \emph{domain-calibrated supervised classifier} trained explicitly on sarcomeric pathogenicity labels under LOGO conditions. These represent fundamentally different inference paradigms, and the comparison should be interpreted not as a contest of ``which model is better'' in a universal sense, but as an empirical study of whether disease-specific calibration provides additive clinical value for high-stakes single-disease deployment.

The domain-calibrated Two-Tower model achieves higher AUPRC than both foundational models on 8 of 9 sarcomeric genes, with a macro-average AUPRC of \textbf{0.8441} compared to \textbf{0.7988} (EVE) and \textbf{0.7996} (AlphaMissense). The margin is most pronounced on genes where explicit structural annotation is rich and training cohorts are moderately sized: \textit{TNNT2} ($+9.9\%$ AUPRC over EVE), \textit{ACTC1} ($+9.5\%$), and \textit{MYL3} ($+17.0\%$). These gains suggest that for well-characterized sarcomeric targets, coupling explicit biophysical constraints (functional-domain occupancy, charge-disruption magnitude, PTM-site proximity) with evolutionary perturbation embeddings provides a level of disease-relevant context that zero-shot foundational models do not natively capture.

However, the comparison reveals a critical and instructive failure mode. On \textit{TNNC1}---the smallest gene in the dataset ($N_{\mathrm{eval}} = 36$)---the supervised model collapses to near-inverse prediction (AUROC $= 0.3500$), while EVE (AUROC $= 0.5962$) and AlphaMissense (AUROC $= 0.6269$) maintain above-chance discrimination. This result is analysed in detail in the Discussion (Section~IV.C) and represents a fundamental boundary condition for supervised LOGO learning under acute data scarcity. It underscores that zero-shot foundational models retain irreplaceable value precisely where disease-specific training data is insufficient to support supervised classification---a complementarity that should inform clinical deployment architectures.

\subsection{VUS Re-stratification Utility}
Applying the trained framework to unresolved variants identified \textbf{4,523} VUS candidates for probabilistic ranking, with a high-confidence subset exceeding predicted pathogenicity thresholds (e.g., $>0.90$). This re-stratification output is not a substitute for clinical adjudication; rather, it is a triage layer that can prioritize expensive functional validation and expedite evidence accumulation for reclassification workflows.

In practice, such ranking can be integrated into molecular tumor-board style or inherited-cardiomyopathy review meetings to sequence laboratory resources toward highest-yield candidates. Because the model is built without ACMG-clinical leakage channels, elevated scores in this context are more interpretable as molecular concern signals instead of restated historical consensus.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Learning Sarcomeric Molecular Grammar Without Clinical Proxies}
The central finding is conceptual: robust out-of-gene pathogenicity prediction is achievable when the model is denied direct access to clinical heuristics and forced to learn from intrinsic molecular descriptors. This supports the idea that sarcomeric proteins carry a learnable ``molecular grammar''---a set of context-dependent substitution rules shaped by evolutionary constraint and biomechanical function.

From this perspective, the two-tower architecture acts as a grammar integrator. The ESM tower captures high-dimensional contextual priors learned from natural protein sequence space; the tabular tower anchors those priors to explicit local biochemical descriptors relevant to contractile mechanics. Fusion then approximates a decision manifold where pathogenicity corresponds to violations of both latent evolutionary plausibility and explicit structural compatibility.

This framing differs from many clinical meta-predictor paradigms. Instead of inferring pathogenicity by aggregating prior human judgments, the model infers pathogenicity by estimating whether a substitution is structurally and evolutionarily incompatible with sarcomere function. The distinction is crucial for translational robustness in under-annotated regions and newly observed variants.

\subsection{Why LOGO Matters for Clinical Translation}
The difference between random-fold success and LOGO success is not merely methodological strictness; it is a test of intended-use validity. In routine deployment, newly reported variants often occur in genes or regions underrepresented in historical labels. A model that depends on intra-gene memorization may degrade sharply under this shift.

By holding out TNNT2 entirely during training, we impose a realistic transfer challenge: can learned rules from other sarcomeric proteins generalize to an unseen gene? The observed AUPRC and confidence interval indicate that transfer is substantial. This does not imply perfect portability across all cardiomyopathy genes, but it demonstrates that leakage-safe learning captures nontrivial shared constraints across the contractile apparatus.

\subsection{The TNNC1 Generalization Boundary}
The near-inverse AUROC of 0.3500 on \textit{TNNC1} warrants dedicated analysis rather than dismissal as a statistical outlier. \textit{TNNC1} encodes cardiac troponin C (cTnC), a 161-amino-acid calcium-sensing regulatory subunit of the troponin complex. Its biophysical and evolutionary profile is fundamentally distinct from the large contractile motor proteins that dominate the training set: \textit{MYH7} (1935 residues, $N=1{,}004$ labeled variants) and \textit{MYBPC3} (1274 residues, $N=435$) are massive, multi-domain mechanochemical engines with heterogeneous tolerance landscapes across motor, converter, lever-arm, and rod regions. \textit{TNNC1}, by contrast, is a compact, hyper-conserved EF-hand protein whose pathogenic mutations cluster in calcium-coordination loops with extremely narrow sequence tolerance.

Under LOGO evaluation, the training set contains zero \textit{TNNC1} variants, forcing the model to transfer rules learned predominantly from motor-protein mutation patterns to a fundamentally different biophysical regime. The resulting failure mode is informative: the tabular features that discriminate pathogenicity in large multi-domain proteins (domain occupancy, positional diversity, secondary-structure heterogeneity) carry different predictive valence in a small, uniformly constrained calcium-binding protein. Rather than producing agnostic predictions, the model systematically inverts its confidence---a signature of negative transfer under extreme distributional shift.

This boundary condition has practical clinical implications. For genes with adequate representation in the training cohort ($N \geq 50$), the supervised Two-Tower model consistently outperforms zero-shot foundational models, providing both higher discrimination and calibrated probability estimates suitable for clinical triage. For ultra-rare gene targets ($N < 40$), clinicians should preferentially consult zero-shot foundational models (EVE, AlphaMissense) that leverage global evolutionary priors independent of the sarcomeric training distribution. We recommend this hybrid deployment strategy---supervised classification for well-powered genes, foundational model deferral for data-scarce targets---as a pragmatic framework for inherited-cardiomyopathy variant interpretation pipelines.

\subsection{The Fusion Trade-off: Discrimination, Calibration, and Inductive Bias}
The ablation results reveal that the architectural role of multi-modal fusion in this framework is not to maximize raw discrimination---which ESM-2 alone achieves most efficiently---but to impose biophysical inductive bias that constrains the prediction manifold to be consistent with known sarcomeric structure-function relationships. In mechanistic terms, the tabular tower encodes immediate substitution chemistry: charge inversion magnitudes, steric mismatch via Grantham distance, functional-domain occupancy, and secondary-structure context. These descriptors directly reflect the physicochemical determinants of actin affinity, ATPase cycle transitions, and thick-thin filament coupling that govern sarcomeric contractile mechanics.

The ESM differential tower, by contrast, encodes a high-dimensional contextual perturbation signature that reflects evolutionary tolerability over hundreds of millions of years of protein-family diversification. This representation is immensely powerful but unconstrained: it captures sequence-context patterns that correlate with pathogenicity without explicit mechanistic grounding, risking overconfident predictions in regions of latent space that are poorly sampled during LOGO training.

Fusion operationalizes a principled trade-off. The tabular branch acts as a regularizing prior that anchors the joint decision boundary to known biophysical laws, preventing the optimizer from exploiting gene-specific distributional artifacts in the ESM space that would not generalize under LOGO transfer. The 2.5-percentage-point AUPRC reduction relative to ESM-only is the measurable cost of this inductive constraint; the improved calibration (Brier score reduction from 0.1893 to 0.1506) is the measurable benefit. For clinical deployment, where predicted probabilities must support thresholded decisions, this trade-off decisively favors the hybrid architecture.

A natural extension is to replace the current concatenation-based fusion with a learned gating mechanism---such as a Gated Linear Unit (GLU) or cross-attention module---that dynamically modulates the influence of each tower conditioned on variant context. Formally, a GLU-based fusion would compute $\mathbf{z}_i^{fus} = \mathbf{z}_i^{tab} \odot \sigma(W_g \mathbf{z}_i^{esm} + \mathbf{b}_g)$, enabling the network to suppress the tabular prior when ESM context is highly informative and amplify it when ESM representations are uncertain. This architectural upgrade is a priority for subsequent work.

\subsection{Interpretability and Hypothesis Generation}
In clinical genomics, interpretability must support decision pathways rather than merely satisfy post hoc curiosity. ISM heatmaps provide actionable interpretability because they produce concrete residue-substitution hypotheses and can be directly cross-referenced with structural/functional annotations. For MYH7, enrichment near converter and actin-interacting regions is biologically coherent with established models of myosin motor dysfunction in HCM \cite{Alamo2017,VanderVelden2022}.

Moreover, ISM can support variant-panel design by identifying substitution classes likely to be deleterious in specific regions, informing targeted saturation experiments. In this way, the predictive framework and wet-lab validation can form a closed-loop discovery system.

\subsection{Clinical Utility of VUS Re-stratification}
The VUS table is best interpreted as a probabilistic prioritization instrument embedded within a broader evidence framework. Potential high-impact use cases include: (i) ranking candidates for deep phenotyping in affected families, (ii) prioritizing variants for functional assay pipelines, (iii) guiding reanalysis cadence in molecular diagnostics labs, and (iv) supporting multidisciplinary review by providing calibrated quantitative evidence.

A leakage-minimized model offers an additional practical advantage: because its predictions are not directly constructed from prior assertion databases, it may provide informative signal precisely where historical evidence is sparse or contradictory. This property is valuable for reducing inertia in long-standing VUS backlogs.

\subsection{Limitations}
This study has several limitations. First, although the publication framing centers a frozen 650M-parameter ESM-2 backbone, embeddings remain frozen and are not fine-tuned to cardiomyopathy-specific objectives. Fine-tuning may increase sensitivity to disease-relevant motifs but also risks overfitting and potential leakage if not carefully controlled.

Second, the architecture does not explicitly model 3D residue-residue graphs or conformational ensembles. Sarcomeric mechanics are inherently structural and dynamic; incorporating geometric deep learning with experimentally derived or predicted structures could capture allosteric effects not represented in current features.

Third, evaluation is strict but still bounded by available labeled data and label quality. Even with de-leaking, ClinVar-derived labels can contain uncertainty, temporal drift, and center-specific adjudication heterogeneity. Prospective external validation across independent clinical cohorts remains essential.

Fourth, ISM interpretations in this implementation are predictive and hypothesis-generating rather than experimentally confirmed causal statements. Structural concordance is encouraging but should be validated with orthogonal biochemical and cellular assays.

Finally, broader deployment will require calibrated threshold governance tailored to use context (research triage versus potential clinical decision support), with explicit integration into ACMG workflows as supportive computational evidence rather than stand-alone diagnostic determinations.

\subsection{Positioning: Global Foundational Models versus Domain-Calibrated Classification}
The benchmarking results in Table~\ref{tab:external_comparison} must be interpreted within a framework that respects the fundamental asymmetry between the compared systems. EVE and AlphaMissense represent landmark achievements in computational biology: EVE distills evolutionary constraint from deep multiple sequence alignments into a principled generative likelihood, while AlphaMissense integrates structural representations from AlphaFold with evolutionary embeddings to achieve proteome-scale variant-effect prediction. Both models operate in a zero-shot regime, requiring no disease-specific labeled data, and their broad utility across thousands of human proteins is well established \cite{Frazer2021,Cheng2023}.

Our framework does not claim to supersede these foundational models. Rather, it addresses a different and complementary question: for a specific high-stakes clinical domain---inherited cardiomyopathy---does the addition of a leakage-free, sarcomere-calibrated classification tower provide actionable value beyond what zero-shot predictions offer? The empirical answer is conditionally affirmative: domain calibration yields higher discrimination and superior probabilistic calibration on 8 of 9 sarcomeric genes, but fails categorically on ultra-rare targets (\textit{TNNC1}) where supervised training data is insufficient.

REVEL and similar meta-predictor ensembles occupy a distinct position in this landscape. While they have provided practical clinical value, their feature ecosystems are intrinsically coupled to prior predictor outputs and clinically adjudicated corpora, creating persistent risk that favorable benchmarks reflect efficient recapitulation of historical adjudication behavior rather than extraction of causal biophysical constraints. In high-stakes inherited-cardiomyopathy settings, this circularity undermines prospective reliability.

Our zero-leakage philosophy should therefore be interpreted as a complementary methodological stance: it forgoes potentially informative but leakage-prone clinical metadata in exchange for cleaner biological attribution and deployment-relevant validation. The central proposition is that predictor credibility for HCM should be evaluated on three axes: feature independence from ACMG-derived evidence channels, gene-level partitioning that prevents homology leakage, and calibration behavior under out-of-gene distribution shift. Future benchmarking in this space should adopt harmonized zero-leakage constraints with explicit documentation of excluded evidence channels and confidence intervals around precision-recall outcomes.

\subsection{Future Directions}
Several extensions are natural. A nested LOGO-plus-temporal validation design could test resilience to both gene and chronology shift. Multi-task learning that jointly models pathogenicity and experimentally measured functional readouts may tighten mechanistic grounding. Structure-aware attention modules or protein graph encoders could be fused with ESM differential embeddings to resolve spatially distant but functionally coupled effects. Uncertainty quantification could be expanded through deep ensembles or Bayesian last-layer approximations to better represent epistemic uncertainty in low-data genes.

Most importantly, prospective laboratory validation of top-ranked VUS candidates should be treated as a first-class objective. A model that reliably enriches true pathogenic variants in assay queues can produce disproportionate clinical value even if not used directly for final classification.

\section{Conclusion}
We present a zero-leakage framework for HCM missense pathogenicity prediction that combines mutation-centric ESM-2 differential embeddings with structural and physicochemical descriptors in a two-tower neural architecture trained with focal loss ($\alpha=0.25$, $\gamma=2$). Under homology-safe LOGO evaluation across nine sarcomeric genes, the hybrid model achieves strong discrimination (AUPRC 0.9151 on TNNT2, 95\% CI 0.8642--0.9649) with clinically actionable calibration (Brier score 0.1506). Ablation analysis reveals that the ESM-only branch provides marginally higher raw discrimination (AUPRC 0.9404), while the tabular biophysical tower contributes essential calibration grounding and domain-specific inductive bias---a trade-off that decisively favors the hybrid architecture for thresholded clinical decisions. Controlled benchmarking against two proteome-scale foundational models demonstrates that disease-specific supervised calibration provides additive value on well-powered sarcomeric genes (macro-average AUPRC: 0.8441 vs.\ 0.7988 for EVE and 0.7996 for AlphaMissense), while identifying a critical generalization boundary on the ultra-rare \textit{TNNC1} target ($N=36$, AUROC: 0.35) where zero-shot models retain predictive capacity. \textit{In silico} mutagenesis analyses translate latent structural knowledge encoded in ESM-2's pre-trained attention maps into actionable residue-level pathogenicity heatmaps aligned with sarcomeric biomechanics. This work supports a methodological shift in inherited-cardiomyopathy AI: from recapitulating clinical guideline artifacts toward learning intrinsic molecular determinants of disease, while acknowledging the irreplaceable complementary role of zero-shot foundational models for data-scarce gene targets.
